# Stylistic variation
**Type:** Behavior  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  
**Also known as:** Tone adjustment  

## State of the Field

The behavior of stylistic variation is framed in two distinct ways across the provided literature. One perspective defines it as rephrasing a report using "different but semantically equivalent expressions" that do not alter the underlying clinical meaning, such as by reordering sentences ("D-CoDe: Scaling Image-PretrainedVLMs to Video via Dynamic Compression and Question Decomposition"). These are described as "purely stylistic or linguistic changes" that are clinically insignificant. A different framing, referred to as "Tone adjustment", characterizes the behavior more broadly as rewriting a given text into a "specific style" or different tonal register as specified by a task ("Principled Personas: Defining and Measuring the Intended Effects of Persona Prompting on Task Performance"). For measurement, stylistic variation is reported to be operationalized using LIWC, with the provided evidence describing a procedure of "length-controlled segmentation" where texts are divided into parts ("MultiMed-ST: Large-scale Many-to-many Multilingual Medical Speech Translation").
