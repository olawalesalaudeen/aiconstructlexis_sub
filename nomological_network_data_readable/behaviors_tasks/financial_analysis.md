# Financial analysis
**Type:** Behavior  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers

## State of the Field

In the surveyed literature, financial analysis is a broad category of behaviors encompassing market prediction, investment decision-making, and risk assessment. A prevalent focus is on stock markets, where the behavior is operationalized as predicting stock price movements—defined as forecasting whether a price will "rise or fall" or have a "positive or negative" direction—and generating trading decisions like "buy, sell, or hold" for individual stocks or entire portfolios. These market-related tasks are performed using inputs such as "historical market information," "historical prices," and textual data like "tweets." A second line of work frames financial analysis through risk classification tasks. This includes credit scoring, which involves classifying individuals as "'good' or 'bad' credit risks"; fraud detection, which categorizes transactions as "'fraudulent' or 'non-fraudulent'"; and financial distress identification, which predicts a company's bankruptcy risk. These classification tasks are typically based on "financial table attributes" or "financial profile attributes." Across these applications, financial analysis is studied in relation to concepts like risk management, decision-making, and safety.
