# Hedging
**Type:** Behavior  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, Hedging is conceptualized in two distinct ways: as a tendency in numerical prediction and as a linguistic style. The first framing defines it as an "observable tendency to produce predictions that are closer to the midpoint (e.g., 0.5) and avoid high-confidence estimates," particularly when an outcome is certain ("Approaching Human-Level Forecasting with Language Models"). One study suggests this behavior may result from safety training and can lead to underperformance in high-certainty scenarios. In contrast, a second line of work treats Hedging as a linguistic phenomenon, defining it as "the use of cautious or non-committal language to soften a statement or minimize imposition" ("MULTIGUARD: An Efficient Approach forAISafety Moderation Across Languages and Modalities"). This perspective frames it as a "negative politeness strategy" that models may overuse. A separate paper also posits a directional relationship, suggesting that the construct of Faithfulness causes Hedging. Depending on the definition, this behavior is operationalized either by analyzing the distribution of numerical predictions or by identifying cautious and non-committal language in generated text.
