## State of the Field

In the provided literature, 'Problem-solving' is characterized both as a general cognitive capability and as a wide array of specific, observable behaviors. Several papers define it abstractly as the ability to devise and execute a strategy to achieve a goal, particularly in complex or unfamiliar domains. However, the prevailing usage operationalizes problem-solving as performance on concrete tasks, which span numerous domains including formal systems like 'algorithmic problem solving' (e.g., sorting lists), 'quantified Boolean formula solving', and 'graph connectivity solving'. Other common instantiations include solving puzzles like Sudoku and crosswords, answering questions in specialized technical fields such as chemistry and circuit analysis, and completing goals in simulated physical environments, termed 'embodied task solving'. Across the surveyed work, this behavior is most frequently measured through performance on standardized benchmarks. The MMLU benchmark is widely cited as an instrument for evaluating both 'world knowledge and problem-solving ability'. Other benchmarks used to operationalize problem-solving include GSM8k, BBH, MathQA, and more specialized tests like PutnamBench and Omni-MATH for advanced mathematics. Conceptually, problem-solving is studied alongside `Mathematical reasoning` and `Algorithmic reasoning`, with one paper noting that mathematical ability is a 'fundamental measure' of this skill.
