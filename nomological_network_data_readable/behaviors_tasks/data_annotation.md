## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, data annotation is broadly characterized as the process of assigning labels or metadata to data, such as text or video, according to a predefined schema. This behavior is operationalized in numerous specific tasks, including assigning preference labels, categorizing text for toxicity or social bias, identifying event arguments, and predicting semantic types for table columns. A prevalent theme is the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) to perform this task automatically, with several sources describing them as an "inexpensive yet powerful way to annotate text" for creating training data. One study even proposes a paradigm where LLMs "autonomously generate, clean, review, and annotate data" for self-training. In contrast, human annotation is also described for more subjective assessments, such as the work on toxicity that distinguishes between "ingroup" annotators, who self-identify with a targeted group, and "outgroup" annotators. A potential issue with LLM-based annotation is also raised, noting that if the model's output is biased, "downstream scientific estimates will also be biased".
