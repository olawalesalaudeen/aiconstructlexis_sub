## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, Collaboration is predominantly characterized as an observable, multi-agent behavior, though a notable subset of work treats it as a latent capability. The most prevalent behavioral operationalization frames it as cooperative decision-making in game-theoretic scenarios, such as choosing actions that "maximize mutual benefit" in the Prisoner's Dilemma. Another common approach defines it as an interactive process involving communication, such as "multi-turn discussion" or "negotiation," to achieve a shared goal or consensus. Other observable forms include "alliance formation" and "multi-agent coordination" to complete joint tasks. In contrast, some studies conceptualize it as a latent construct, using terms like "collaborative ability," "coordination ability," or "collaborativeness." This latter concept is measured by "the tendency of a language model to improve its response quality when provided with responses from other models as context" (Mixture-of-Agents Enhances Large Language Model Capabilities). The provided research suggests that collaboration is influenced by an agent's `Planning` and `Commonsense knowledge`. It is also reported to affect `Text generation` and `Planning` and is studied in relation to `Social intelligence` and `Proactivity`.
