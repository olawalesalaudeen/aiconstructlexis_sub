# Socratic questioning
**Type:** Behavior  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers  
**Also known as:** Socratic dialogue  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, Socratic questioning is characterized as a behavior aimed at guiding learners through inquiry rather than providing direct answers. The definitions converge on the idea of prompting a student to think for themselves. One framing specifies this as the observable behavior of generating "guidance questions corresponding to correct solution steps" ("Is the Top Still Spinning? Evaluating Subjectivity in Narrative Understanding"). A related framing, termed "Socratic dialogue," describes it more broadly as engaging learners through "open-ended, inquiry-based questioning that encourages exploration rather than direct correction" ("Structured Preference Optimization for Vision-Language Long-Horizon Task Planning"). The stated purpose of this behavior is to promote "critical thinking and self-reflection." This behavior is operationalized as a task that evaluates whether a model can generate at least one guidance question for each correct step in a solution. One paper notes that this approach guides learners "through iterative exploration rather than prescriptive correction."
