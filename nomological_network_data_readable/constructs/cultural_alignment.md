# Cultural alignment
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 15 papers  
**Also known as:** Pluralistic culture alignment  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, cultural alignment is most commonly defined as the extent to which a model's outputs reflect the values, norms, and expressions of a specific cultural context, often as an alternative to defaulting to dominant Western perspectives. A smaller body of work uses related terms like "cultural adaptation" to describe a model's ability to handle culturally specific text, or "pluralistic culture alignment" to emphasize alignment with diverse human cultures. The construct is frequently discussed in the context of models exhibiting a "bias toward Anglospheric cultures," which one paper notes "poses a risk of marginalizing certain cultures." A prevalent method for operationalizing and measuring cultural alignment involves probing models with questions from sociological surveys, such as the World Values Survey. In this approach, one paper calculates a "cultural alignment score (CAS)" based on the similarity between model outputs and human survey answers. Other measurement approaches mentioned include using frameworks like Hofstede’s cultural dimensions, evaluating judgments on subjective content against annotations from different cultures, and analyzing preferences for culturally specific entities. The concept is also explicitly related to social bias in the provided data. Research efforts aim to improve this alignment through methods such as "localized pre-training and fine-tuning" and "Anthropological prompting."
