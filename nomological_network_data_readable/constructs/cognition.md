# Cognition
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 8 papers  
**Also known as:** Cognition capability  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, 'Cognition' is a construct with several distinct definitions depending on the research context. The most prevalent framing, particularly in vision-language studies, defines it as the latent ability to integrate perceptual information with domain-specific knowledge to reason about images and deduce complex answers. Other work defines it differently, such as the capacity to effectively understand and use long-form text, or, in the context of embodied agents, the ability to interpret one's own actions and their consequences. To operationalize and measure this construct, papers commonly employ the MME benchmark, which is described as testing for both perception and cognition competencies across 14 subtasks that require simple 'yes' or 'no' answers. The relationship between cognition and perception is a recurring theme, with one definition explicitly involving the integration of 'perceptual information' and the MME benchmark assessing both constructs. Cognition is also studied alongside fine-grained understanding.
