# Plasticity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 9 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, plasticity is consistently defined as a model's ability to adapt to new information. The prevailing usage frames this as adapting to a "new data distribution, such as a different domain or language," while a smaller line of work treats it as the "acquisition of new knowledge and skills." The effectiveness of this adaptation is a recurring theme, with one paper specifying it as the ability to "reach target performance with minimal steps or to achieve the highest possible performance" (DEPT: Decoupled Embeddings for Pre-training Language Models). Operationalization of this construct varies; one approach measures it by the "improvement of CER on new cases," quantifying how many new tasks a model can solve compared to its baseline (UniRAG: Unified Query Understanding Method for Retrieval Augmented Generation), while other work associates it with specific metrics like AP4 and FWT4. The concept is frequently discussed in the context of a "stability-plasticity trade-off," where the ability to learn new information is contrasted with the retention of old knowledge. Finally, some research studies plasticity alongside the construct of Safety.
