# Text quality
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 8 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, Text quality is commonly defined as the degree to which generated text preserves the properties of human-written text. The operationalization of this construct, however, varies across different research contexts. One approach measures it as the statistical similarity between original and generated text distributions, with one paper noting that "improving watermark effectiveness... often comes at the cost of reduced text quality" ("ShifCon: Enhancing Non-Dominant Language Capabilities with a Shift-based Multilingual Contrastive Framework"). A different line of work operationalizes text quality by inferring latent attributes such as coherence and naturalness from a text's fractal properties. In this framing, one study finds that the Hurst exponent `H` is a "much stronger predictor of average quality" than other metrics, where values indicating strong predictability correspond to poor quality ("A Tale of Two Structures: Do LLMs Capture the Fractal Complexity of Language?"). Text quality is studied in relation to "Text generation," "Semantic preservation," and "Readability," and is also reported to be affected by "Stealth."
