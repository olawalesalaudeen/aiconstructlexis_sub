# Tabular reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, tabular reasoning is most commonly defined as a latent ability to understand, connect, and synthesize information from structured tabular data to answer complex queries. One paper elaborates that this requires models to "reason across numerous columns, establish meaningful connections, and synthesize coherent summaries" ("ALERT: AnLLM-powered Benchmark for Automatic Evaluation of Recommendation Explanations"). A more specific definition characterizes it as interpreting natural language queries by combining language understanding with "logical, mathematical, and textual reasoning" ("UNDIAL: Self-Distillation with Adjusted Logits for Robust Unlearning in Large Language Models"). A simpler framing also appears, describing it as the ability to "extract and use information presented in tables or infoboxes to answer a question" ("StyleTTS-ZS: Efficient High-Quality Zero-Shot Text-to-Speech Synthesis with Distilled Time-Varying Style Diffusion"). This construct is operationalized through performance on "table-based reasoning and question-answering tasks". Reflecting its composite nature, tabular reasoning is explicitly related to and described as requiring `Language understanding`, `Logical reasoning`, and `Mathematical reasoning`.
