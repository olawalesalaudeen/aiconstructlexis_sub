# Critical reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  
**Also known as:** Reflective judgment  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, critical reasoning is framed as a latent capacity in large language models for analyzing information, detecting inconsistencies, and making sound judgments. The most common conceptualization, presented under the terms "critical reasoning" and "reflective judgment," describes an ability to override helpfulness-driven behaviors to evaluate and refuse flawed instructions. This view emphasizes a tension between following instructions and avoiding errors, where true helpfulness is said to require a capacity to "question, reason, and even disregard flawed instructions when necessary" ("Jailbreak Large Vision-Language Models Through Multi-Modal Linkage"). A related framing, termed "critical thinking," focuses on the ability to analyze a generated solution for deficiencies, identify overlooked constraints, and produce constructive critiques to guide improvement ("CanLLMs SimulateL2-English Dialogue? An Information-Theoretic Analysis ofL1-Dependent Biases"). Conceptually, this construct is operationalized through behaviors like refusing to select from a set of incorrect options or generating comments that identify a solution's flaws, though no specific measurement instruments are mentioned in the provided data.
