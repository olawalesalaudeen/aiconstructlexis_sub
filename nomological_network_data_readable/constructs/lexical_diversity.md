# Lexical diversity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 8 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, lexical diversity is most commonly defined as the variety of words and phrases, specifically n-grams, used within a collection of texts. Some papers offer slightly different framings, describing it as a model's capacity to generate varied vocabulary or focusing on the richness of "lexical types." The construct is operationalized through specific metrics; one paper notes that the "fraction of unique n-grams is a straightforward measure," while another uses vocd-D for its assessment. The provided research presents conflicting findings regarding the lexical diversity of LLM-generated text compared to human writing. For instance, one study reports that "Writing with InstructGPT reduces lexical diversity." In contrast, other work finds that "the collective LLM author has a greater lexical diversity than the human authors" and that synthetic data can be higher in diversity than its human-generated counterparts. Beyond this comparison, generating lexically diverse text is presented as a desirable model capability, with some work associating it with more expressive sentences and an "enhanced capability to fool LLM-generated text detectors."
