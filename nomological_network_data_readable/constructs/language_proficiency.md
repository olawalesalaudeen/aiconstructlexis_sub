## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, 'Language proficiency' is a broad construct used to describe two distinct sets of capabilities: one centered on the mastery of formal linguistic rules, and another focused on commonsense and causal reasoning about the world. The most prevalent framing defines it as a latent ability for understanding and applying rules of syntax, semantics, and morphology, with terms like 'linguistic competence' and 'grammatical knowledge' being common. A substantial parallel line of work, however, defines it as the capacity for reasoning, encompassing abilities like 'physical commonsense reasoning', 'causal reasoning', and even 'local cultural reasoning'. A few papers offer more specific framings, such as 'figurative competence' or 'knowledge memory', which is described as a "deeper form of memory corresponding to functional linguistic competence." The construct is operationalized in varied ways corresponding to these definitions. The formal linguistic aspect is frequently measured through grammatical acceptability judgment tasks, where models must distinguish between correct and incorrect sentences, using benchmarks such as BLiMP and SyntaxGym. In contrast, the commonsense and causal reasoning facets are typically assessed with question-answering datasets like PIQA and COPA. Language proficiency is studied alongside other constructs such as `causal inference` and `knowledge recall`, and a few papers report that it influences behaviors like `knowledge transferability`.
