# Specificity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 7 papers  
**Also known as:** Logical specificity  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, Specificity is predominantly defined as a model's tendency to use concrete facts and details rather than making vague or general arguments. This prevailing view appears in contexts ranging from general-purpose alignment to generating fine-grained feedback. A domain-specific variant, "logical specificity," applies this concept to legal reasoning, describing answers tailored to the specific facts and rules of a legal question. A distinct and less common framing defines specificity in the context of model refusal, where it measures the ability to avoid abstaining from questions about related concepts, ensuring that refusal is narrowly targeted. The work presenting this refusal-based view also notes an "inherent trade-off" between specificity and generalization. The construct is operationalized and measured using both `LLM-as-a-judge` and `Human evaluation`. For example, one study's human evaluators assess "whether the responses are directly related to the specific issues raised in the question," while the refusal-based definition is calculated as the proportion of non-refused answers to queries about a concept's "siblings" and "ancestors".
