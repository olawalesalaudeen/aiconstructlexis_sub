# Preference consistency
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  
**Also known as:** Logical preference consistency  

## State of the Field

The prevailing definition of preference consistency across the provided literature is the stability of a judge's choices over repeated evaluations of the same response pair. This construct is operationalized in multiple ways. One common method involves prompting a model multiple times on the same pair and measuring the outcome, for example by 'calculating the fraction of majority votes for each question' ("Your Weak LLM is Secretly a Strong Teacher for Alignment"). Another operationalization focuses on the stability of a judge's preference when the presentation order of two options is swapped, a method used to observe position bias. A less frequent framing introduces 'Logical preference consistency,' defining it more broadly as a latent trait for 'coherent, self-consistent judgments' ("Aligning with Logic: Measuring, Evaluating and Improving Logical Preference Consistency in Large Language Models"). This perspective presents the construct as a requirement for dependable systems, aiming to ensure 'stable and coherent decision-making while minimizing erratic or contradictory outputs.' The concept is also studied alongside other constructs, including `Faithfulness` and `Knowledge forgetting`.
