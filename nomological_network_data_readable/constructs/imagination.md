# Imagination
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 5 papers  
**Also known as:** Spatial imagination  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, imagination is most commonly described as a cognitive ability for mental simulation and visualization, particularly in the spatial domain. This is frequently specified as "spatial imagination," the ability to mentally manipulate spatial relationships or visualize the outcomes of transformations, such as a physical process or code execution. A different framing treats imagination as the generation of novel visual representations, specifically "intermediate subgoal images," to guide planning and action. Another view emphasizes its role in inferring missing perceptual knowledge, with one study noting that model performance "degrades when required to imagine a scene after changes before reasoning" (Hypo3D). The construct is operationalized through tasks like 3D visualization problems in coding, where failure to, for example, apply the Right-Hand Rule is attributed to "limited visual spatial imagination" (Can MLLMs Reason in Multimodality? EMMA: An Enhanced MultiModal ReAsoning Benchmark). A single paper also posits a causal link, suggesting imagination influences creativity.
