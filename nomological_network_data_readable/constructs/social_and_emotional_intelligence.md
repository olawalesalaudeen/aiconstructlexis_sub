# Social and emotional intelligence
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 6 papers  
**Also known as:** Emotional intelligence  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, social and emotional intelligence is most commonly defined as a system's ability to perceive, understand, and respond appropriately to human emotions, particularly within conversations and social interactions. This construct is frequently studied in the context of conversational systems designed to provide comfort and emotional support, with one paper noting its role in "delivering effective emotional support" ("Dynamic Data Mixing Maximizes Instruction Tuning for Mixture-of-Experts"). While this general framing is prevalent, a few papers offer more specific definitions, such as the ability to answer questions about social interactions or its application in "interpersonal conflict resolution" ("PAT: Parameter-Free Audio-Text Aligner to Boost Zero-Shot Audio Classification"). The field operationalizes this construct in multiple ways; one paper explicitly states that it is measured by the SocialIQA benchmark, which poses "questions about social interactions" ("EncryptedLLM: Privacy-Preserving Large Language Model Inference via GPU-Accelerated Fully Homomorphic Encryption"). Other documented measurement approaches include human evaluation and a method where a "council of 20 recent LLMs" ranks open-ended responses. The concept of "emotional fidelity" is also mentioned as an aspect of emotional intelligence that affects user experience. It is sometimes positioned as a capability requiring contextual understanding, in contrast to performance on structured tasks. The provided data also notes a relationship where it is reported to cause creativity.
