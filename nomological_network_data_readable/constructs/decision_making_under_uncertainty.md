# Decision-making under uncertainty
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers  
**Also known as:** Decision making under uncertainty  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, "Decision-making under uncertainty" is characterized in two distinct ways, both centered on handling incomplete information. The most common framing defines it as the capacity to choose the more likely outcome when evidence is "partial, ambiguous, or incomplete" (BIRD: A Trustworthy Bayesian Inference Framework for Large Language Models). This is operationalized through tasks where a model must "decide which outcome is more likely" given some context. A different line of work treats it as the "ability to iteratively search for optimal solutions by evaluating possibilities based on limited evidence and prior beliefs," particularly in settings with vast solution spaces (Searching for Optimal Solutions with LLMs via Bayesian Optimization). Both definitions agree that uncertainty arises from "incomplete task information." One source describes the construct as a "hallmark of intelligent behavior," while another suggests that improvements in this area "contribute to better and more trustworthy decision making." The construct is also studied in relation to Faithfulness, as seen in the work "DeLLMa: Decision Making Under Uncertainty with Large Language Models".
