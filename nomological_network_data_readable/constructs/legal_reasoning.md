# Legal reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 14 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, the most prevalent definition of legal reasoning is the latent ability to interpret legal texts, apply rules, and draw legally grounded conclusions. While this broad framing is common, other papers offer more specific conceptualizations, with some emphasizing structured, logical inference, noting that "legal reasoning of-ten relies on a well-defined logical flow" (Diverse In-Context Example Selection After Decomposing Programs and Aligned Utterances Improves Semantic Parsing). A smaller set of work highlights the need to understand "implicit rules" and adhere to formal procedures like "legal syllogism" (Pula: Training Large Language Models for Setswana). The field operationalizes and measures this construct using benchmarks that assess performance on component tasks. The primary instrument cited is LegalBench, which is used to evaluate "various aspects of legal reasoning" across numerous tasks. These tasks are specified to include abilities such as "issue-spotting, rule-recall, rule-application, rule-conclusion, interpretation, and rhetorical understanding" (SaulLM-54B & SaulLM-141B: Scaling Up Domain Adaptation for the Legal Domain), as well as statutory interpretation and structured explanation generation. Legal reasoning is also studied in relation to Specificity, with some research observing that model-generated responses often "lack logical specificity". It is also studied alongside Logical reasoning.
