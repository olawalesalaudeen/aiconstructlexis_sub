# Semantic diversity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 5 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, semantic diversity is consistently defined as the extent to which generated content varies in meaning. The definitions show slight variations: one framing emphasizes that outputs should remain "contextually appropriate" while reflecting a "broader exploration of the response space" ("Controlling Large Language Model with Latent Action"), while another highlights "richness and unpredictability in conceptual expression" ("Blind Men and the Elephant..."). As a construct, it is operationalized by measuring semantic similarity, where lower similarity across outputs indicates greater diversity. For example, one study notes that human-written texts "exhibit a lower mean similarity than most models, indicating greater semantic diversity" ("Blind Men and the Elephant..."). The construct is presented as an outcome that can be influenced by other model capabilities. One paper reports that improved "Controllability" through latent actions can "enable greater semantic diversity" ("Controlling Large Language Model with Latent Action"). Another paper posits that "Semantic understanding" causes semantic diversity, though no direct evidence for this is provided in the snippets. Overall, semantic diversity is treated as a desirable quality of text generation that is actively evaluated and targeted for improvement.
