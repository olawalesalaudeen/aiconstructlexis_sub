# Syntactic competence
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  
**Also known as:** Formal competence, Syntactic generalization  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, Syntactic competence is characterized as a latent ability of language models concerning grammatical and structural rules. The definitions vary in their emphasis: one frames it as the ability to "correctly process and judge grammatical structures in accordance with native speaker intuitions" ("Speech Vecalign: an Embedding-based Method for Aligning Parallel Speech Documents"), while another, termed "Formal competence," focuses on applying syntactic rules "independent of its meaning" ("Trustworthy Medical Question Answering: An Evaluation-Centric Survey"). A third perspective, "Syntactic generalization," defines it as the capacity to apply grammatical patterns to "novel or rare constructions" not seen during training ("2025.emnlp-main.1399.checklist"). Research investigates this construct to evaluate the "robustness of LMs’ syntactic abilities," particularly concerning parameters like "word order and subordination" ("Speech Vecalign..."). This construct is operationalized and measured using benchmarks such as BLiMP and SyntaxGym. It is also studied in relation to Language proficiency.
