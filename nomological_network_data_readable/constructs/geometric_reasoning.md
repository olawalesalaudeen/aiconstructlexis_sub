# Geometric reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 5 papers  
**Also known as:** Geometric comprehension, Geometric understanding, Geometric ability, Geometric structure understanding  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, geometric reasoning is most commonly defined as the ability to perform multi-step, symbolic inference using information from both geometric figures and textual descriptions to arrive at a solution. This broader capacity is often discussed alongside component abilities like "geometric comprehension" or "understanding," which refer to the perception and interpretation of basic geometric elements, their properties, and spatial relationships. The construct is operationalized through geometry problem-solving tasks, and one paper introduces the GeomRel dataset to specifically measure the "understanding of geometric structures" by isolating the core step of identifying relationships, which is described as the "foundation of reasoning ability" ("Do Large Language Models Truly Understand Geometric Structures?"). Geometric reasoning is frequently studied in the context of Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs), with several sources noting that these models exhibit limitations in this area. The ability is situated within a network of related constructs, described as a form of "visual mathematical reasoning" ("G-LLaVA: Solving Geometric Problem with Multi-Modal Large Language Model") that requires "spatial reasoning" and is also associated with relational and abstract reasoning. Efforts to improve this ability in models involve creating specialized "geometric instruction data" ("G-LLaVA: Solving Geometric Problem with Multi-Modal Large Language Model") and addressing insufficient Chain of Thought training data.
