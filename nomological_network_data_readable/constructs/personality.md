# Personality
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 15 papers  
**Also known as:** Agreeableness, Openness to Experience, Honesty-Humility, Extraversion, Emotional Stability, Honesty  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, Personality is treated as a latent construct, most commonly defined as a configuration of enduring traits that shape a model's expressive and social responses. The prevailing operationalization of this construct is through the HEXACO personality framework, which papers use to assess dimensions such as Honesty-Humility, Emotionality, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Openness to Experience. A recurring theme is the connection between these traits and model safety; several papers report that adjusting traits like Agreeableness, Openness to Experience, and Honesty-Humility can reduce bias and toxicity in generated text. For example, one study notes that "high levels of Agreeableness and Honesty-Humility in particular help reduce LLM bias" ("Multimedia Event Extraction withLLMKnowledge Editing"). While most definitions focus on latent tendencies, a slightly different framing of "Honesty" appears in one paper, where it is defined behaviorally as the act of "acknowledging limitations by declining to provide an answer" to minimize hallucinations ("ViClaim: A Multilingual Multilabel Dataset for Automatic Claim Detection in Videos"). The literature also distinguishes between the stability of different traits, with one source suggesting that Agreeableness is "highly susceptible to user influence" while Emotional Stability remains "relatively more stable" ("Bias Mitigation or Cultural Commonsense? EvaluatingLLMs with aJapanese Dataset").
