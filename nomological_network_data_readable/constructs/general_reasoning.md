# General reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 14 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, general reasoning is most commonly framed as a broad, unobservable cognitive ability for logically processing information and solving problems in non-specialized domains. Several papers describe it as a capability where current models excel, noting their "remarkable proficiency." A more specific definition, however, characterizes it as the latent ability to solve individual tasks without necessarily capturing the "common patterns or generic facts" that underlie multiple examples. This framing is used to explicitly contrast it with abstract reasoning, with one paper highlighting a "substantial discrepancy" between the two performances. This theme of juxtaposition is common, as general reasoning is also contrasted with performance on specialized tasks that require domain-specific knowledge. While papers refer to improving performance on "general reasoning tasks," the provided data does not specify how this construct is operationalized or measured.
