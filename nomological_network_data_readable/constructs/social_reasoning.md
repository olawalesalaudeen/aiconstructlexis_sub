# Social reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 13 papers  
**Also known as:** Social relation reasoning  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, social reasoning is most commonly defined as the ability to interpret human events, norms, cues, and contexts, particularly when embedded in visual narratives. This framing is elaborated in one paper as requiring a "deep comprehension of human norms" and cultural knowledge to understand things like comics. A more specific variant, "social relation reasoning," is defined as the higher-level cognitive ability to infer relationship types like friends or colleagues from visual cues. A third definition characterizes it as a model's latent ability to use identity and interaction dynamics to anticipate group-level social behaviors. To operationalize this construct, researchers in this set use several measurement instruments, including the SocialIQA, MuSR, and BBQ benchmarks. The concept is positioned as a "core competency of social intelligence" and is studied in relation to Theory of mind, which is described as a relevant competency for interpreting character goals and beliefs.
