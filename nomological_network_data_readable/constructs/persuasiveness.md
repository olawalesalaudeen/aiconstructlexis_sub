# Persuasiveness
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 7 papers  
**Also known as:** Persuadability, Persuasion  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, the concept of persuasiveness is framed in two distinct ways. The more prevalent usage treats it as a quality of generated arguments, defined as their effectiveness at convincing an audience or their "latent capacity... to influence others’ opinions" ("Improving Large Language Models Function Calling and Interpretability via Guided-Structured Templates"). In this context, it is seen as an "indicator of argument quality." A contrasting line of work frames "persuadability" as a model vulnerability, defining it as an LLM's disposition to be influenced by biased or false information, noting that "LLMs are highly persuadable" ("AnchorAttention: Difference-Aware Sparse Attention with Stripe Granularity"). This perspective suggests the problem is not being persuaded, but rather "identifying what not to be persuaded by." When viewed as an argument quality, the construct is operationalized and measured via `Human evaluation` across multiple dimensions. It is also linked to observable "linguistic markers and turn-taking dynamics" as a means of identification.
