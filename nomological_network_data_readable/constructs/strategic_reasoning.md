# Strategic reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 10 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, strategic reasoning is most commonly defined as the ability to analyze competitive situations, anticipate opponent actions, and formulate plans within rule-based, multi-agent environments. This prevailing view is often applied to game-theoretic contexts, such as "board and card games that require pure logic and strategic reasoning to compete with opponents" (GTBench: Uncovering the Strategic Reasoning Capabilities of LLMs via Game-Theoretic Evaluations). A more specific framing describes it as the ability to analyze an "opponent's policies and behavioral patterns in order to formulate an effective counter-strategy" (Hypothetical Minds: Scaffolding Theory of Mind for Multi-Agent Tasks with Large Language Models). In contrast, a less common perspective broadens the concept to include navigating "dynamic environments and align[ing] long-term goals amidst uncertainty," as seen in scenarios like business negotiations. To operationalize this construct, researchers in this set use measurement instruments such as SOTOPIA and TTT-Bench. The concept is also studied alongside other constructs, including data analysis, decision-making, and theory of mind.
