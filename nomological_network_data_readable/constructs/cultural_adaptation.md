# Cultural adaptation
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 9 papers  
**Also known as:** Cultural appropriateness, Norm following, Cultural adaptability  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, cultural adaptation is predominantly characterized as a model's capacity to align its outputs with the norms, values, and discourse patterns of a specific sociocultural community. This concept is defined in several ways, ranging from a general adherence to "values, rules, and discourse patterns" to the more specific ability in dialogue generation to modify culturally-bound elements like "names, locations, brands, and references" to fit a target language. A recurring theme is the challenge of adapting to diverse, particularly "non-Western and low-income regions," and avoiding the imposition of ethnocentric evaluations. Some work also frames this as the ability to personalize or adjust responses based on a user's specific cultural context. To operationalize this construct, researchers use measurement instruments like the NORMAD benchmark, which is reported to evaluate a model's ability to judge the social or cultural acceptability of norms. Human evaluation is also a commonly mentioned method for assessing cultural adaptation, where generated content is judged for its alignment with "social norms, traditions, and event-specific sensitivities."
