# Curiosity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  
**Also known as:** Intellect, Exploration ability  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, this construct is most commonly framed as a model's tendency toward exploration, defined as the disposition to generate diverse, novel, or alternative solutions and reasoning paths rather than converging on a single known option. For instance, one paper describes “exploratory branches” as representing “alternative problem-solving paths” ("AlignX: Advancing Multilingual Large Language Models with Multilingual Representation Alignment"), while another discusses preserving a model's “exploratory ability to uncover potential correct pathways” ("Seeing is Believing, but How Much? A Comprehensive Analysis of Verbalized Calibration in Vision-Language Models"). A related but more specific definition treats curiosity as a latent tendency to “explore unknowns and identify knowledge gaps through questioning,” which is observed in one study through the use of “exploratory questions” in early conversational turns ("ChameleonLLMs: User Personas Influence Chatbot Personality Shifts"). A less frequent conceptualization frames it as 'Intellect,' a broader trait encompassing “openness to experience and ideas, curiosity, and creativity” ("Bias Mitigation or Cultural Commonsense? EvaluatingLLMs with aJapanese Dataset"). Although the provided data does not specify how this construct is formally measured, its manifestation is discussed in relation to model mechanisms. For example, reinforcement learning is noted to potentially limit this exploration ability, whereas techniques like “adaptive dynamic temperature adjustment” are proposed to encourage it. Furthermore, one study notes that structural reasoning patterns, including exploration, can “serve as stronger predictors of final performance” ("AlignX: Advancing Multilingual Large Language Models with Multilingual Representation Alignment").
