# Reasoning robustness
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 6 papers  

## State of the Field

The concept of reasoning robustness is framed in at least two distinct ways across the provided literature. One line of work, focused on mathematical reasoning, defines it as the extent to which a model's reasoning remains correct when a problem is altered through rewriting, the addition of irrelevant information, or reframing. This view is operationalized by testing models on an original problem and its variants, which are designed to probe "problem understanding, irrelevant disturbance, and scenario understanding" using instruments like the MATHCHECK checklist. A different conceptualization, presented in the context of sports question answering, defines reasoning robustness as an LLM's ability to maintain reliable performance when handling multiple, interdependent questions within a single input. This latter perspective associates the construct with a model's "knowledge boundary awareness" and "improved confidence estimation" as means to reduce hallucination.
