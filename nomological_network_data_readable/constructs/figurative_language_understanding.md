# Figurative language understanding
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers  

## State of the Field

Based on the provided data, figurative language understanding is defined as the latent ability to interpret non-literal utterances, such as irony or hyperbole, by reasoning about rhetorical strategies. This construct is operationalized and studied through tasks like sarcasm detection and metaphor detection. One paper explicitly frames sarcasm and metaphors as "two types of figurative language" that are part of a broader effort to detect "complex linguistic phenomena." The literature also describes a specific modeling approach, the "(RSA)2 framework," which addresses this ability by considering a "speaker’s employed rhetorical strategy." This method is noted to enable "human-compatible interpretations of non-literal utterances" and re-establishes a focus on modeling the "communicative goals of speakers and listeners."
