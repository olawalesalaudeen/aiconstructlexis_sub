# Generation diversity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 85 papers  
**Also known as:** Diversity, Distinctness, Content diversity, Question diversity, Response diversity, Prediction diversity, Solution diversity, Plan diversity  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, generation diversity is predominantly characterized as a model's capacity to produce a wide variety of distinct and non-repetitive outputs. This concept is frequently defined at multiple levels, encompassing lexical variety (avoiding repetitive n-gram patterns), semantic distinctness (generating different ideas), and structural variation (using different phrasing or reasoning paths). The construct is operationalized through a range of measurement instruments; papers report using n-gram-based metrics such as Distinct-n, Self-BLEU, and n-gram repetition rates, as well as methods like calculating "average semantic similarity between stories" or using "predictive entropy across the responses" (`Language Models as Implicit Tree Search`). While the focus is most commonly on model outputs, a smaller line of work treats diversity as a property of input data, such as "Question diversity" in training sets, or as a cognitive ability termed "Divergent thinking." A recurring theme is a trade-off between diversity and other qualities, with several papers discussing the need to balance it against alignment and coherence. For instance, one study notes that RLHF "significantly reduces output diversity compared to SFT" (`Understanding the Effects of RLHF on LLM Generalisation and Diversity`). The construct is studied in relation to text generation and uncertainty quantification, and is reported by some authors to influence generalization and faithfulness.
