# Clinical reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, clinical reasoning is consistently defined as a latent ability that involves applying medical knowledge and logical inference to patient scenarios. The definitions agree that this capacity goes beyond simple factual recall, integrating elements like clinical judgment, context, and pathophysiology. One definition specifically includes the ability to interpret clinical data and perform "structured calculations" ("Can Vision-Language Models Solve Visual Math Equations?"). The papers discuss its assessment in different contexts. For instance, one study questions whether "MCQ-based evaluations" are sufficient, suggesting they "may inadequately assess LLMs’ medical knowledge and clinical reasoning skills" ("Geometric Signatures of Compositionality Across a Language Model’s Lifetime"). Another line of work operationalizes this construct through medical calculations, which are described as "structured, high-stakes reasoning in clinical workflows." This involves sub-tasks like "selecting the correct formula, extracting clinical variables, and performing numerical computations" ("Can Vision-Language Models Solve Visual Math Equations?").
