# Knowledge reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 9 papers  
**Also known as:** Knowledge and reasoning  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, knowledge reasoning is consistently defined as a latent ability involving the use of factual, external, or stored world knowledge to answer questions through inference. The definitions commonly emphasize two components: the application of factual knowledge and a reasoning process over that knowledge to derive an answer, with one source describing it as "understanding and reasoning over factual and conceptual knowledge" (RAG-Zeval: EnhancingRAGResponses Evaluator through End-to-End Reasoning and Ranking-Based Reinforcement Learning). To measure this construct, researchers operationalize it using a range of evaluation benchmarks. The most frequently cited instruments for assessing knowledge reasoning in this dataset are MMLU, ScienceQA, ARC, WinoGrande, and CommonsenseQA. One paper specifies that MMLU is used to "assess the model’s comprehensive knowledge reasoning abilities" through its multiple-choice questions spanning numerous subjects (Constructions are Revealed in Word Distributions). Within the research landscape presented, knowledge reasoning is often categorized alongside other cognitive abilities, most commonly appearing with "commonsense reasoning" and "mathematical reasoning." The construct is also studied in relation to knowledge manipulation.
