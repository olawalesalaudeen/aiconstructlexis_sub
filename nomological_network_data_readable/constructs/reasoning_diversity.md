# Reasoning diversity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 5 papers  

## State of the Field

Reasoning diversity is consistently defined across the provided literature as a model's capacity for, or production of, distinct reasoning strategies rather than repeating similar solution patterns. While one source frames it as an observable output, another describes it as a "latent capacity" of the model to generate varied reasoning paths. The construct is operationalized by empirically examining multiple solutions from a model to assess their variation, with a lack of diversity indicated when "later solutions often repeat earlier ones" (Do NOT Think That Much for 2+3=? On the Overthinking of Long Reasoning Models). Reasoning diversity is studied in relation to other model behaviors; for instance, it is negatively associated with "Overthinking," which is characterized by a "lack [of] diversity in reasoning strategies." Some research analyzes its limited contribution to accuracy, while other work proposes methods like "Diversified Thought" to enhance it, aiming to improve "path diversity" and reduce "redundant patterns" in model outputs.
