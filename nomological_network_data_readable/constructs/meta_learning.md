# Meta-learning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers  
**Also known as:** Intrinsic metacognitive learning  

## State of the Field

The provided literature presents two distinct conceptualizations of meta-learning. One framing defines it as the ability to learn how to perform a new task by inferring its rules from a few examples, a capacity associated with in-context learning that is contrasted with simply "retrieving or copying information from the context." A different perspective, termed "intrinsic metacognitive learning," describes it as a latent capacity for an agent to autonomously refine its own learning strategies through self-assessment, self-planning, and self-evaluation. This view emphasizes an agent's "intrinsic ability to actively evaluate, reflect on, and adapt its own learning processes" without reliance on fixed, external mechanisms. The construct is operationalized through its relationship with other capabilities. Specifically, the capacity for intrinsic metacognitive learning is reported to cause `Generalization`, as it allows an agent to "autonomously and adaptively refine their learning strategies in response to shifting tasks and domains." It is also reported to cause `Self-correction`.
