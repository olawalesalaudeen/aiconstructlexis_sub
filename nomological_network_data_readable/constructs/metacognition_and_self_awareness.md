## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, 'Metacognition and self-awareness' refers to a broad set of latent abilities concerning a model's capacity to monitor, understand, and regulate its own internal states, knowledge, and cognitive processes. The most prevalent framing centers on a model's awareness of its own knowledge boundaries, often defined as 'self-knowledge'—the ability to distinguish what it knows from what it does not to avoid unsupported claims. This includes related concepts such as the 'knowledge boundary,' described as the limit of factual understanding beyond which a model may hallucinate, and 'capability awareness,' the assessment of its own skills for a task. A second major line of work conceptualizes this construct as the capacity to reason about reasoning itself, termed 'meta-reasoning' or 'metacognition,' which involves self-monitoring and verifying the correctness of its own cognitive processes. These constructs are operationalized through observable behaviors such as a model's ability to refuse to answer when it detects inconsistencies, its capacity to self-correct errors, or its decisions to use external tools when internal knowledge is deemed insufficient. A smaller set of studies explores more specific facets, such as 'self-recognition,' the ability to identify its own generated text based on an internalized 'writing style knowledge,' or 'resource awareness,' which involves adapting strategies based on external constraints. While the terms 'metacognition' and 'self-awareness' are used widely, the field employs a diverse and granular vocabulary—including 'knowledgability,' 'reasoning boundary,' and 'response quality awareness'—to characterize specific aspects of this internal monitoring.
