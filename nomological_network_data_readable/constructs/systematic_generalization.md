# Systematic generalization
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 7 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, systematic generalization is most frequently defined as the ability to solve novel problems by composing knowledge learned from multiple, smaller training examples. A related framing, sometimes termed 'compositional generalization', describes this as reusing and combining learned components to solve novel combinations of known skills, while another definition characterizes it as applying learned abstract structures to new instances. The construct is operationalized using benchmarks where, as one paper notes, "the test instances are systematically made larger than the informationally complete training instances." Specific measurement approaches mentioned in the data include using the GrailQA benchmark and tasks that test whether models can "solve addition problems recursively." Systematic generalization is studied in relation to a wide array of concepts, including abstract reasoning, length generalization, knowledge transfer, and mathematical reasoning. Some papers propose directional relationships, suggesting that in-context learning may lead to systematic generalization, and that systematic generalization, in turn, is reported to be a "main driver" of generalization in certain contexts. It is also discussed alongside multimodal understanding and safety, and one paper states it is "believed to underlie" human abilities for abstraction.
