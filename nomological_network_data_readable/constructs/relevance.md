# Relevance
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 38 papers  
**Also known as:** Relation, Contextual relevance, Topic relevance  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, Relevance is predominantly framed as the degree to which a model's generated response is pertinent and on-topic with respect to a user's prompt, question, or task context. This is frequently described as the model's ability to stay focused, with one paper noting the ideal is when "every piece of information [is] contributing to a comprehensive understanding of the topic" (AutoSurvey: Large Language Models Can Automatically Write Surveys). While this on-topic framing is prevalent, a few papers offer more specific conceptualizations, such as "Topic relevance" for arguments in a debate or "Contextual relevance" for retrieved information beyond surface similarity. One study also defines it mathematically as a balance between two other metrics, akin to an F-score. The field operationalizes and measures this construct most commonly through human and automated judgment. A frequent approach is using "LLM-as-a-judge," where models like GPT-4 are prompted to rate responses on a numerical scale for their relevance. Similarly, human evaluation is widely employed, with annotators assessing whether a response "directly addresses the question." A smaller set of studies uses automated metrics, such as BERTScore to measure if a summary captures key information or CLIP Score to quantify text-image correspondence. Relevance is often studied as a dimension of instruction-following and is evaluated alongside other attributes like coherence, fluency, and accuracy.
