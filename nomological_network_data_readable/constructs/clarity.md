# Clarity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 15 papers  
**Also known as:** Writing clarity  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, Clarity is most commonly defined as the quality of a model's output being understandable, well-structured, and free from ambiguity. The most frequent definition, appearing in seven papers, frames it as the extent to which an output is "unambiguous and clearly formulated, enabling precise interpretation." While this core idea is prevalent, some papers introduce additional facets; for instance, one study includes "linguistically fluent," and another extends the concept to encompass the "layout of the presented information" in addition to language. A smaller set of sources offer more specific framings, such as defining clarity for moderation comments as using "plain, simple, and clear language" to avoid overwhelming the user. The construct is operationalized in the literature primarily through human evaluation, with several papers reporting the use of multi-point rating scales to assess it. For example, one paper evaluates clarity for generated questions by asking annotators, "Is the question clear? (not ambiguous)" ("Achieving Human Parity in Content-Grounded Datasets Generation"). Clarity is frequently assessed as one of several subjective quality dimensions, often studied alongside accuracy, relevance, conciseness, and adherence to instructions.
