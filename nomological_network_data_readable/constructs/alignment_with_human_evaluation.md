# Alignment with human evaluation
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers  
**Also known as:** Judging accuracy, Alignment with human judgments  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, 'Alignment with human evaluation' is consistently defined as the degree of correspondence between the judgments of an automated system, such as an LLM evaluator or user simulator, and those of human annotators. This construct is operationalized by measuring the correlation or agreement between the automated system's ratings and human-provided scores or preferences. While this core definition is shared, some work also refers to this concept as 'judging accuracy' and expands the reference standard to include not only human judgments but also those from 'strong AI'. One paper explicitly connects this alignment to a model's 'reliability as a reward model', framing it as an 'ability to judge' that can be improved. Researchers use this construct to assess how well different evaluation methods align with human evaluations, with one study noting that the 'most useful checklist creation method varies across different evaluation models and tasks' for achieving this alignment.
