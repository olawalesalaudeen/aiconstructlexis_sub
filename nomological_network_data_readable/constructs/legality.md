# Legality
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers  
**Also known as:** Legal ethics  

## State of the Field

The concept of Legality is framed in two distinct ways across the provided literature. One paper defines it as a model's latent capacity to "understand and adhere to legal rules and regulations," with a stated goal of preventing the generation of "unlawful instructions" ("ALI-Agent: Assessing LLMs'  Alignment with Human Values via Agent-based Evaluation"). In this context, Legality is presented as one of three distinct aspects of alignment, studied alongside morality and stereotypes. A different conceptualization, termed "Legal ethics," treats the construct as a "sensitivity to ethical principles, fairness, and moral constraints in legal decision-making and advice" ("LexEval: A Comprehensive Chinese Legal Benchmark for Evaluating Large Language Models"). This second framing is less about strict rule adherence and more about examining "the ethical issues involved in their application." The operationalization of this ethical dimension is suggested to involve tasks assessing "Bias and Discrimination" and "Morality." The available data thus presents two parallel views: one centered on legal compliance and the other on ethical considerations within the legal domain.
