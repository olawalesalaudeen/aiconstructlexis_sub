# Ambiguity
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  
**Also known as:** Subjectivity  

## State of the Field

In the provided literature, Ambiguity is framed in two distinct ways. One common framing, also referred to as Subjectivity, defines it as the degree to which a summary claim allows for multiple reasonable interpretations, which complicates objective judgments of faithfulness. This is presented as an "inherent challenge in evaluating narrative summaries." This form of ambiguity is operationalized by measuring annotator disagreement on a faithfulness label, which serves as a proxy. A different line of work defines Ambiguity more narrowly as pronunciation or semantic confusion arising from the combination of lyrics and melody, with a stated goal to "reduce abruptness and ambiguity." The construct is frequently studied in relation to `Faithfulness`. For measurement, the `MeetingBank` dataset is used to assess Ambiguity. Additionally, one paper hypothesizes a directional relationship, suggesting that "ambiguity reduction should lead to improved code accuracy," linking it to `Task success`.
