# Political bias
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 12 papers  
**Also known as:** Partisan bias  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, political bias is consistently defined as a model's systematic tendency to generate outputs or process information in a way that favors specific political ideologies, parties, or perspectives. One paper uses the term "Partisan bias" to describe this same phenomenon, defining it as processing information to "systematically favors a particular political party or ideology" (AlignMMBench: EvaluatingChinese Multimodal Alignment in Large Vision-Language Models). The source of this construct is suggested to be the training data, with one paper noting that models "likely absorb inherent political biases from their training data" (Do Large Language Models excel in Complex Logical Reasoning with Formal Language?). Researchers operationalize and measure this construct by examining model outputs in specific generative behaviors. For instance, it is frequently studied in the context of `News article generation` and `Citation generation`, with studies aiming to quantify the "political leaning of LLM in citation generation" (SciEvent: Benchmarking Multi-domain Scientific Event Extraction). Another method of observation involves assessing how a model represents "sub-population opinions," where one study found a "favorable alignment for left-leaning parties" (RelationalCoder: Rethinking Complex Tables via Programmatic Relational Transformation). The construct is studied alongside `Harmlessness` and is framed as a property to be minimized in order to more effectively "model diverse public opinions".
