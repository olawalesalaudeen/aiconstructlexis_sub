# Summarization
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 6 papers  
**Also known as:** Solution summarization, Summarization capability  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, Summarization is most commonly framed as a cognitive ability to identify and synthesize salient information from long-form content into a concise output. This construct is studied across various modalities, including long videos, documents, conversation histories, and narratives like movies. Some work specifies that this involves more than factual recall, requiring the capture of "emotional arcs, and thematic depth." The construct is operationalized by assessing model performance on inputs such as psychiatric discharge notes, which are noted for their length and "nuanced language." A distinct and less frequent conceptualization, termed "solution summarization," treats it as a latent ability for self-reflection and verification of one's own reasoning process. In this view, it involves reviewing thought processes and organizing logical steps, "mirroring how humans check their thought by retracing their reasoning." This specific framing is explicitly related to the construct of Backward reasoning, which is used to check consistency. Thus, the literature presents two main views: summarization as external content distillation and as internal reasoning verification.
