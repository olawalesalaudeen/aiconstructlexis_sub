# Anthropomorphism
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 5 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, anthropomorphism is conceptualized in at least two distinct ways, both concerning the attribution of human qualities to non-human entities. One definition frames it as the "effectiveness" of attributing "coherent human-like characteristics, emotions, and motivations" to artificial entities while ensuring their "original nature" is preserved. A different perspective, explicitly proposed as a new definition for LLM-centered systems, describes anthropomorphism as a "reciprocal phenomenon." This latter view posits that the construct emerges from an interaction between designers who "embed human-like cues into artifacts" and users who "project their cognitive response to the cues onto the artifacts" ("Few-Shot Learning Translation from New Languages"). This framing is grounded in the idea that LLMs occupy a "liminal space between utilitarian tools and social actors." The provided data does not contain information on how this construct is operationalized or measured.
