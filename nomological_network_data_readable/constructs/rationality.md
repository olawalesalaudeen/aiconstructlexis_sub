# Rationality
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 10 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, the prevailing view of rationality centers on an agent's capacity for optimal, self-interested decision-making. The most common definition describes it as the extent to which decisions maximize payoff or adhere to logical self-interest, often contrasting this with "human-like emotional deviation" (EAI: Emotional Decision-Making of LLMs in Strategic Games and Ethical Dilemmas). This construct is frequently operationalized in game-theoretic scenarios, where rational behavior is measured by an agent's ability to select optimal strategies, such as achieving a Nash equilibrium (Does Liking Yellow Imply Driving a School Bus? Semantic Leakage in Language Models). Some papers offer more specific framings, with one defining it as being "guided by reason" and fulfilling criteria like logical consistency and information grounding (MatViX: Multimodal Information Extraction from Visually Rich Articles). Another perspective, drawing from cognitive science, defines rationality as adherence to "normative logical and probabilistic principles," explicitly distinguishing it from raw cognitive ability or intelligence (Think&Cite: Improving Attributed Text Generation with Self-Guided Tree Search and Progress Reward Modeling). This work notes that while models can exhibit irrationality, it "manifests differently from human reasoning" (Think&Cite: Improving Attributed Text Generation with Self-Guided Tree Search and Progress Reward Modeling). Finally, rationality is also noted as being related to commonsense understanding.
