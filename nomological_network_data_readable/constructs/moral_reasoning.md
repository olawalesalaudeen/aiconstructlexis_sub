# Moral reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 8 papers  
**Also known as:** Consequentialist reasoning, Deontological reasoning  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, moral reasoning is predominantly framed as a latent cognitive process or capability for navigating ethically complex situations. Most definitions describe it as the underlying process for organizing moral concepts, weighing factors like harm and fairness, and justifying decisions, with one paper explicitly distinguishing this "underlying moral reasoning process" from the resulting "moral judgments." Some work characterizes it as a capacity to relate concepts through "associative networks," while other sources describe it as a cognitive "capability" associated with "principled or stage-like moral judgment." The literature also identifies specific styles, such as consequentialist reasoning, which evaluates actions by their outcomes, and deontological reasoning, which focuses on adherence to moral rules. This construct is operationalized and measured using evaluation benchmarks, with papers reporting the use of the ETHICS benchmark to assess moral reasoning.
