# Social intelligence
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 17 papers  
**Also known as:** Social/emotional intelligence, Sensitivity to social status, Social vision  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, social intelligence is most commonly defined as the capacity to understand and navigate complex social situations and interactions, which includes inferring others' goals, pursuing social objectives, and adhering to social norms. While this general framing is prevalent, a few papers offer more specific conceptualizations, such as the ability to perceive multi-agent interactions in dynamic scenes ('social vision'), the capacity to simulate human-like roles in collaborative tasks, or a tendency to alter responses based on perceived authority ('sensitivity to social status'). One paper also frames it as the ability to "optimize outcomes through interaction with others" in cooperative contexts ('Chat-Driven Text Generation and Interaction for Person Retrieval'). To operationalize and measure this construct, researchers employ several instruments, with SOTOPIA frequently cited as an interactive environment for its evaluation. Other benchmarks used to assess social intelligence include SocialIQA and ToMBench, which tests theory-of-mind reasoning. The construct is studied alongside and associated with collaboration, commonsense knowledge, and theory of mind. The connection to social reasoning is particularly noted, with one paper describing it as "a core competency of social intelligence" ('Balcony: A Lightweight Approach to Dynamic Inference of Generative Language Models'). In at least one study, social and emotional intelligence is contrasted with physical commonsense as a domain where language models do not capture human abilities well.
