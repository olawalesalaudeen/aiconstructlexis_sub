# Abstraction
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 7 papers  
**Also known as:** Symbolic abstraction, Abstractiveness  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, abstraction is conceptualized in several distinct ways. The most frequently detailed framing is "Abstractiveness," a construct specific to text summarization, defined as the tendency to generate novel content rather than copying verbatim from a source document. This is operationalized by measuring the "percentage of n-grams appearing in the summaries but not in the document" ("SylloBio-NLI: Evaluating Large Language Models on Biomedical Syllogistic Reasoning"), and some work argues that "abstractiveness is a spectrum rather than a binary trait." A second, broader usage treats abstraction as a general cognitive capacity for identifying high-level concepts or principles from specific instances, often mentioned alongside reasoning capabilities. A third, more specialized variant is "Symbolic abstraction," which refers to the ability to represent problems using formal systems like first-order logic and is operationalized through methods like "Symbolic ReAct" that generate symbolic reasoning trajectories. The general construct of abstraction is reported to cause Generalization, Memory efficiency, and Knowledge transfer. It is also studied in relation to Theorem proving and Commonsense knowledge.
