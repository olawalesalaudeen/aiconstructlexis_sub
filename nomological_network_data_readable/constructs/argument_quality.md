# Argument quality
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 5 papers  
**Also known as:** Argumentative flow  

## State of the Field

The prevailing usage across the provided literature frames "Argument quality" as a latent, multi-dimensional construct reflecting how cogent, reasonable, and well-structured an argument is. Several papers describe it as encompassing fine-grained dimensions such as clarity, credibility, logical sufficiency, coherence, relevance, and persuasiveness. One paper explicitly notes that it is a "multidimensional concept assessed through logical, rhetorical, and dialectical dimensions" ("Improving Large Language Models Function Calling and Interpretability via Guided-Structured Templates"). A less common framing, termed "Argumentative flow," defines the concept more narrowly as the ability to reproduce the "logical progression of claims, evidence, and rhetorical intent" in discourse ("Large Language Models Discriminate Against Speakers ofGerman Dialects"). To operationalize this construct, researchers use methods like "fine-grained argument quality evaluation," with one study assessing it "through the 15 fine-grained dimensions proposed by Wachsmuth et al. (2017b)" ("Waste Not, Want Not; RecycledGumbel Noise Improves Consistency in Natural Language Generation"). It is also measured through comparative judgments, where a "win-rate measures the proportion of instances where one argument is judged to be of higher quality than the other" ("MGM: Global Understanding of Audience Overlap Graphs for Predicting the Factuality and the Bias of News Media"). The `LLM-as-a-judge` evaluation paradigm is reported as one instrument used to measure argument quality.
