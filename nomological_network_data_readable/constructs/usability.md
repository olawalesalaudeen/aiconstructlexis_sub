# Usability
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 3 papers  
**Also known as:** User-centricity, Patient-centricity  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, Usability is characterized in several distinct ways, often focusing on how well a system serves its intended users in specific contexts. A prevalent framing, termed "User-centricity," defines it as a quality that prioritizes the user's experience, specifically regarding "the interpretability of its results and the user's ability to interact with and control the process." This perspective is reinforced by the construct's stated relationship with both `Interpretability and explainability` and `Interactivity`, with one paper noting that single-turn systems suffer from limited user-centricity. Other definitions present a more application-specific view, such as its role in enabling effective use in "real documentation workflows." A specialized variant, "Patient-centricity," adapts the concept to healthcare, measuring the degree to which model responses prioritize a patient's individual needs and context. To operationalize this construct, papers report using `G-Eval` for measurement. In the healthcare context, it is also evaluated as a metric alongside fluency, empathy, and ROUGE-L scores.
