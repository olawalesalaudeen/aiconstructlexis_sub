# Structural reasoning
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 12 papers  
**Also known as:** Structured reasoning  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, structural reasoning is most commonly defined as the ability to reason in a way that respects formal constraints and multi-step dependencies, particularly in planning problems. This framing emphasizes a logical, step-by-step process to arrive at a conclusion. A different line of work provides a more specific definition, characterizing it as the ability to follow and generate complex, non-linear reasoning paths, such as those structured as trees or graphs. This view is supported by connections to structural Chain-of-Thought methods like Tree-of-Thought (ToT) and Graph-of-Thought (GoT), and by frameworks that model reasoning as a "graph of interconnected reasoning steps." The construct is operationalized and measured using benchmarks like ARC. Additionally, some research introduces specific instruments to assess it, such as the PERTURBQA benchmark, which evaluates reasoning "over semantically-rich graphs, in the context of molecular biology." Structural reasoning is studied in relation to code generation, language proficiency, and graph partitioning. One paper also proposes that it causes or contributes to interpretability and explainability.
