# Critical thinking
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 5 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, critical thinking is characterized in two distinct ways. One framing, presented in "OlympicArena: Benchmarking Multi-discipline Cognitive Reasoning for Superintelligent AI", positions it as a component of logical reasoning, defining it as the general ability to objectively analyze and evaluate information to form a reasoned judgment. A different characterization, from "Reward-Shifted Speculative Sampling Is An Efficient Test-Time Weak-to-Strong Aligner", describes it as a latent model capability for independently evaluating conflicting viewpoints, resisting incorrect user influence, and reaching correct conclusions. This work suggests that models with "independent critical thinking capabilities" are better able to "identify correct answers across varying perspectives." The development of this ability is linked to reducing model biases and enhancing performance on various downstream tasks. While both definitions emphasize forming reasoned judgments, they differ in scope, with one being a general cognitive skill and the other a specific model property related to autonomy. The provided data does not specify how this construct is operationalized or measured.
