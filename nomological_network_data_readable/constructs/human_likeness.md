# Human-likeness
**Type:** Construct  
**Referenced in:** 15 papers  
**Also known as:** Human-like comprehension, Perceived humanlikeness  

## State of the Field

Across the surveyed literature, "Human-likeness" is most commonly defined as the degree to which a model's response patterns resemble human distributions, explicitly contrasting this with the goal of achieving high task accuracy. As one paper states, the focus is on "comparing models and humans on their response patterns, not their absolute performance" (DevBench: A multimodal developmental benchmark for language learning). However, several other framings exist. A smaller set of studies treats it as a perceptual quality, where "perceived humanlikeness" affects a user's willingness to engage with and rely on an agent. Other work defines it based on the quality of generated behavior, such as its naturalness and coherence, or as an internal cognitive capacity for "human-like comprehension." A few papers also extend the concept to the learning process itself, calling for models with "human-like learning trajectories" (Eliciting Critical Reasoning in Retrieval-Augmented Generation via Contrastive Explanations). The construct is operationalized in various ways, including evaluations of a model's `Emotional expression` to assess the "realism and believability of the character’s portrayal" (Effective Skill Unlearning through Intervention and Abstention). It is also measured by assessing qualities like the naturalness of conversational responses and the statistical resemblance of generated text to an "authentic human-written distribution" (MUZO: Leveraging Multiple Queries and Momentum for Zeroth-Order Fine-Tuning of Large Language Models).
