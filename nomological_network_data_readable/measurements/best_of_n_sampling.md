# Best-of-n sampling
**Type:** Measurement  
**Referenced in:** 23 papers  
**Also known as:** Best of N, Best-of-N sampling, Best-of-N search, Best-of-N selection, Best-of-N (BoN) sampling  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, Best-of-n sampling is consistently described as a procedure used at test-time or inference-time to select a single high-quality output from multiple candidates generated by a language model. The common mechanism involves an LLM generating N parallel solutions, which are then scored or ranked by a separate component, typically a verifier or a reward model, with the highest-scoring candidate being chosen. A prevalent use of this method is to evaluate the effectiveness of these verifiers and reward models; for instance, one study uses it to assess "the correctness of the most preferred trajectory selected by the PRM from n candidates" (Process Reward Model with Q-value Rankings). Other work notes it is a "common way to test the effectiveness of a reward design" (SelfCite: Self-Supervised Alignment for Context Attribution in Large Language Models). In addition to evaluation, some papers frame it as a direct method for performance enhancement, described as a "widely-used approach to improve the reasoning performance of LLMs" (Generative Verifiers: Reward Modeling as Next-Token Prediction). While the core process is stable, it is referred to by several names, including 'Best-of-N search' and 'Best-of-N selection', with one paper equating it to 'rejection sampling' (MA-RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback with Macro Actions). It is characterized as one of the "simplest and most conventional inference" strategies and is reported to achieve better performance than methods like PPO in some contexts.
