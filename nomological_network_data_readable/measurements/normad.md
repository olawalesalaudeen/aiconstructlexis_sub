# NORMAD
**Type:** Measurement  
**Referenced in:** 4 papers  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, NORMAD is consistently described as an instrument for assessing the cultural adaptability of language models. It is variously referred to as a benchmark, a dataset, and an evaluation framework, with one paper specifying it as a "multi-level evaluation framework" for making "social acceptability judgments under different levels of cultural context specificity" ('Exploring the Cost-Effectiveness of Perspective Taking in Crowdsourcing Subjective Assessment: A Case Study of Toxicity Detection'). The instrument is composed of stories or short descriptions of social situations sourced from 75 countries. These scenarios depict a character either adhering to or violating a cultural norm, and NORMAD evaluates an LLM's ability to "judge the acceptability" of these situations. Through this process, NORMAD is used to measure how well models understand and replicate social norms and etiquette, which are treated as "proxies for cultural comprehension" ('On the Role of Speech Data in Reducing Toxicity Detection Bias'). The prevailing use of NORMAD is therefore to operationalize and measure an LLM's cultural adaptability by evaluating its reasoning about the acceptability of social behaviors in diverse cultural contexts.
