# FactScore
**Type:** Measurement  
**Referenced in:** 35 papers  
**Also known as:** FActscore, FActScore, FACTSCORE, FACTSCORES, FActScore-Atom, FactScore-Bio  

## State of the Field

Across the provided literature, FactScore is consistently described as an evaluation instrument—variously termed a procedure, benchmark, tool, or metric—for assessing the factuality of long-form text from LLMs. The prevailing operationalization, which appears in nearly all sources, is a 'decomposition-then-verify' framework where generated text is broken down into 'atomic facts' or 'claims'. Each claim is then individually verified against a knowledge source, which is often specified as Wikipedia, retrieved passages, or citations. The output is typically a quantitative score, such as the 'percentage of atomic facts supported' or a precision score. The relationships show that FactScore is most frequently used to measure the construct of `Faithfulness`, and in one instance, `Hallucination`. While its application is for long-form text generally, the most commonly cited use case is for evaluating the factual correctness of biography generations. A few papers also introduce or discuss variants, such as FactScore-Atom, a modified decomposition policy, and FactScore-Bio, a question-answering dataset.
